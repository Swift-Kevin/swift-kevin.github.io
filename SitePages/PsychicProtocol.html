Team Role: UI/Generalist Programmer

Team Size: 6

Development Time: 2 months

User Interface (UI)
  • UI was one of the many responsibilities I held during this project where I communicated with other teammates to ensure consistent functionality.
    •  
  • Inspiration for the player status bars came from Dishonored. Similar to how inspiration for the main menu came from Portal.
    •  
  • All game menus are controller supported which when reflecting on the project turns out to be more tedious than initially thought.
Radial Wheel
  • One of my most proudest parts of the project, a large majority of my work was spent on the Radial Wheel.
    •  
  • The radial wheel works by calculating the distance and angle of the mouse's position from the center of the screen and then highlights and updates the corresponding ability.
    •  
  • When an ability is selected it sends a update to the players current ability and updates the UI accordingly.
    •  
  • While I was working on the Radial Wheel, a lot of lessons were learned. One of which was overcomplicating a simple feature by going outside of the requirements of said feature. This fell under "What would you do differently" during our post-mortem.
Displaying Credits
  • I wanted to give proper credit to all the people whos assets we used in our game, this was unfortunately uncommon within similar student projects and that was one thing I wanted to address.
    •  
  • One of the major inspirations for the games credits was movie theater credits, typically the ones scrolling from bottom to top with a fade-in and fade-out.
    •  
  • In my implementation of the credits menu, I wanted to make it modular so I could keep adding to the list of assets and proper. That and also putting our Git Commit history (skippable) at the very end to show our progress throughout the project.
The Main Menu
  • When creating the Main Menu, the idea given was a "simple live scene". I took that and thought of adding randomly switching rotating security cameras that zoomed in and out as a cool gimick of the scene.
    •  
  • During our Beta Sprint (2nd Month, 3rd week) I felt like the Main Menu was empty. I asked the Team Lead about it and after some discussions on approaching it, we agreed to have a randomly rotating enemy in the middle.
    •  
  • Fun Fact: The mysterious being (floating rotating enemy model) soon became known within our game universe's God that was spinning perpetually in place in the main menu, potentially providing the powers given to the player.
Checkpoint Notifications
  • A radial-filled notification displays in the bottom right of the screen to let the player know about a checkpoint being reached.
    •  
  • When a checkpoint is reached the next room is loaded and the previous is unloaded, similar to object pooling which is a design method used to increase a games performance.
Player Settings
  • I used scriptable objects to help quickly access data for the player settings within menus aswell as across scenes.
    •  
  • When changing a setting the player must 'Apply' the changes as to confirm them or they can back out of the menu or press cancel, both resulting in reverting the saved data.
    •  
  • Since more than just player settings were stored in the scriptable object, getters and setters were commonly used within the functionality associated with the object as to retrieve stored data and update it.
Audio Implementation
  • Almost all entities in the game have an attached audio source customized to its own purpose, an example being the death sound effect of enemies is setup in 3D space.
    •  
  • The team wanted to have spatial audio due to it avoiding the "flatness" of non-spatial audio. Awareness was important to the player and their decision making.
    •  
  • A neat effect I learned about when implementing audio was the use of a lowpass filter to create a muffled effect on music when inside of game menus.
Reticle
  • The color swaps depending on what you are looking at. Red for enemies, Green for telekinetic objects, white for non-tagged objects.
    •  
  • Priority of color is assigned by distance, whichever object is hit by the reticles raycast is shown.
    •  
  • One of the features I tried to accomplish with reticles was changing the icon for it, unfortunately this feature never 100% worked.
Directional Hit Indications
  • Calculated by taking the angle between an incoming projectile and the players forward.
    •  
  • There are 5 hit-indication images in an array that are cycled through and displayed on a pivot around the reticle.
    •  
  • When the player is hit, the screen borders also have a vignette effect colored the type of resource being consumed (health is red, shield is blue).
Elevators and Scene Transitions
  • In order to progress within the game, the player must get to the elevator at the end of the level and then activate the elevator proceeding to the next level.
    •  
  • One challenge that I faced was getting the elevators button to disappear after not hovering over it. After hours of me and a friend attempting to solve it, my friend Tom (Team Lead) walked by and suggested to store the variable. (It worked).
    •  
  • Timers were used frequently within the elevators as fading the scene in and out to provide a seamless transition between scenes.
